Plotting a Sequence#

Linear Functions and Arithmetic Sequences#

In general, we recognize sequences with constant change as “linear” functions. We can define these by thinking recursively and using the representation $\(f(n) = \text{starting value} + \text{change} \times n\)$

For example, the sequence \(a_n = [3, 5, 7, 9, 11, 13]\), we have:

\[\text{starting value} = 3\]
\[\text{change} = 2\]

thus;

\[f(n) = 3 + 2n\]

Slope and Intercept#

The change of a linear function is often talked about as the slope of the line. In general, given any two points on a line – \((x_1, y_1)\) and \((x_2, y_2)\) – , the slope is the average difference between those points:

\[\text{slope} = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1}\]

and by rearranging this equation you can write an equation for the line as:

\[y = \text{slope} \times (x - x_1) + y_1\]

For example, suppose you want an equation between the points \((3, 5)\) and \((8, 12)\).

First we determine the slope \(\frac{12 - 5}{8 - 3} = \frac{7}{5}\), then plug into our equation $\(y = \frac{7}{5}(x - 3) + 5\)$
